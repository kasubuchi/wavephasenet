Hallucination Theory 4 Q&A
Q1 What does it mean that hallucination is a "mathematical inevitability" rather than a training problem? ▾
Key Point

The paper's central claim is that LLM hallucination does not arise from insufficient training data or imperfect optimization. It is a structural consequence of the mismatch between the mathematical framework LLMs operate in (sequential approximation of conditional expectations over a σ-additive family) and the nature of real-world truth sets.

Authors' Position

The semantic space of an LLM is non-complete. Specifically:

The semantic space is deformed at each Transformer layer, locally distorted by Attention, and dynamically reconstructed by context. As a result, it cannot be treated as a fixed metric space with a well-defined notion of completeness. It does not converge, lacks limit points, changes shape with context, and has unstable distances — all hallmarks of a non-complete space.

This "semantic drift" and "inference runaway" are the natural consequences of the non-completeness. The truth set (the set of correct real-world propositions) has a non-convex Čech-type consistency structure, while the LLM embedding space is linear and convex — this fundamental mismatch is the root cause of hallucination.

Q2 Isn't "truth set is non-convex, embedding space is convex, therefore hallucination is inevitable" a circular argument without proof? ▾
AI's Critique

The claim that the truth set is non-convex is itself unproven. The identification of the LLM output space with the convex structure of an Lp space is arbitrary. "Convex space → false propositions" is not a proof. The real causes of hallucination are distributional approximation error, data sparsity, and epistemic uncertainty.

Authors' Response

The paper does not argue that "convex space causes false propositions." This is a misreading.

Section 2.3 uses the Minkowski inequality to demonstrate that the embedding space supports linear superposition of contexts, which is the source of generative flexibility — not directly of hallucination.

Embedding vector space (finite-dimensional Hilbert space over ℝ) is complete — this is not in dispute.

The semantic space, however, is a dynamic space formed by chains of reasoning, association, and knowledge. Attention's nonlinear, stochastic, context-dependent nature means the space cannot be treated as a fixed complete metric space.

That the truth set has a non-convex Čech-coherent structure is addressed in Section 3.2 — this is a topological consistency argument, not a simple convex/non-convex comparison.

Q3 What exactly does the definition "an output that is optimal over a σ-additive family but does not belong to the truth set" mean? ▾
Mathematical Summary
· Truth set: \(T \subset V^*\) — the set of correct real-world propositions
· Model-optimal set: \(\arg\min_f \mathbb{E}[l(f(X), Y)]\)
· Hallucination: output in the model-optimal set but not in \(T\)

An LLM sequentially approximates conditional expectations over the σ-additive family generated by past token sequences (\(\mathcal{F}_t = \sigma(x_1,\ldots,x_t)\)). This yields average optimality over a probability distribution, not a guarantee of truth. This structural divergence makes hallucination inevitable.

Why the "insufficient data" theory is incorrect

Some reviewers suggest hallucination stems from insufficient information. The paper explicitly refutes this: even a perfectly trained model with complete data will hallucinate, because the structural mismatch between the truth set and the embedding space persists regardless of data quantity. This is why the problem becomes critical in AI agents requiring strict logical consistency.

Q4 In what sense is the LLM's semantic space "non-complete"? Isn't the embedding space a Hilbert space and therefore complete? ▾
Authors' Explanation

The embedding vector space (a finite-dimensional Hilbert space over ℝ) is indeed complete. The semantic space that emerges during inference is a distinct entity.

LLM inference (via Attention) is nonlinear, stochastic, and context-dependent. The space is deformed as it passes through each layer. Since the destination of inference changes with context, temperature, sampling, and Attention weights, the concept of a "limit point" is ill-defined.

Consequently the semantic space:
· does not converge
· has no well-defined limits
· changes shape with context
· has unstable distance metrics

These are precisely the characteristics of a non-complete space.

WavePhaseNet addresses this non-completeness through the lens of dynamical systems, Riemannian manifolds, measure spaces, homotopy-theoretic spaces, and Laplace-transform-type analysis.

DFT & Semantic Decomposition 3 Q&A
Q5 Is there a mathematical basis for "low-frequency components = global meaning / intent"? Or is this merely an assumption? ▾
AI's Critique

There is no guarantee that meaning concentrates in low-frequency components. Negation and conditionals can disrupt low-frequency structure. "Low-frequency = meaning" is a hypothesis, not a theoretical necessity. The DC component is merely the sequence-wise mean; claiming it encodes "summary" requires additional assumptions.

Authors' Response

This critique overlooks prior empirical work. The correspondence between low-frequency DFT bands and semantic intent has been demonstrated in the prior work FNet (Google Research), which is cited in Section 10 of the paper.

Mathematical guarantee of the DC component (k=0):
The DC component in DFT is by mathematical definition the lowest-frequency component — the sequence-wise mean. Interpreted over token embedding vectors, this component contributes uniformly to every position.

This is why it is mathematically guaranteed to correspond to sentence-level summary, intent, and topic — properties that are, by definition, independent of individual token positions.

Negation and conditionals are local syntactic structures — they manifest as high-frequency variations in the DFT domain and are handled by the high-frequency module. Separating them from the global intent signal (low-frequency) is precisely the goal of SCHS construction.

Q6 Isn't the "perfect reconstruction" in the numerical example (d=4, N=3) just the trivial identity property of DFT/iDFT? It doesn't demonstrate the method's effectiveness. ▾
AI's Critique

The numerical example is a textbook DFT calculation. "Perfect reconstruction" is merely the trivial identity property of DFT followed by iDFT — it does not demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Authors' Response

The perfect reconstruction example (Section 6) serves a specific purpose: demonstrating that DFT itself is mathematically sound as a minimal instance of the general theory. The paper states this explicitly (Section 6.1).

The essential value of WavePhaseNet lies not in the reversibility of DFT per se, but in the ability to insert frequency-dependent semantic operators between DFT and iDFT:

\(X \xrightarrow{\mathrm{DFT}} \hat{X} \xrightarrow{M(k)} \tilde{\hat{X}} \xrightarrow{\mathrm{iDFT}} \tilde{X}\)

Section 7 demonstrates with concrete numbers that after applying the frequency mask \(M(k)\), the inverse transform yields an embedding matrix semantically distinct from the original — preserving intent while suppressing syntactic variation. This is fundamentally different from the identity transform.

Dimensionality Reduction (24,576 → 3,000) 3 Q&A
Q8 How is the reduction from 24,576 to ~3,000 dimensions theoretically justified without empirical measurements? ▾
AI's Critique

No empirical data is provided. There are no actual GPT embedding spectral plots. The cumulative energy curve is based on theoretical assumptions alone. Neither the theory nor the empirics support the claim. The derivation of 3,000 dimensions is insufficiently rigorous.

Authors' Response

This is a mathematical theory paper. The approach is: establish the theory first, then empirically validate it — consistent with how mathematical physics papers are structured.

Derivation logic:

① Natural language follows Zipf's law and is self-similar
→ Energy spectrum of embedding amplitudes decays as \(E(f) \propto 1/f^\alpha\)

② Via the Yule–Simon process, eigenvalues decay in the limit of this scale-free distribution

③ For GPT-4 scale (d = 24,576), syntactic constraints + LayerNorm/Attention regularization push \(\alpha \in [1.1, 1.4]\)

④ At this \(\alpha\), cumulative energy of 82–88% corresponds to K ≈ 3,072

⑤ Therefore ~3,000 dimensions is the lower bound for preserving meaning and intent

The 3,000 figure is not heuristically chosen — it is derived from the scale-invariant structure of the Zipf + Yule–Simon framework. Empirical validation is the next step, not a prerequisite for the theory.

Q9 GPT-4's internal dimensionality is not publicly disclosed by OpenAI. Is it valid to use 24,576 as a premise? ▾
Authors' Position

The paper explicitly treats this as an assumed value. Section 8.3.1 states: "we explain the theoretical development using the latest 24,576-dimensional embedding σ-additive family space" — this is presented as a hypothetical example, not an empirically verified fact.

The core of the theoretical argument is the relationship between dimensionality d and spectral exponent α. The specific value d = 24,576 serves as an illustrative example. The general framework applies to any d, and the derivation of α ∈ [1.1, 1.4] follows from the structural properties of language models at GPT scale — not from d = 24,576 specifically.

Q10 Why exactly is 3,000 the "lower bound"? What breaks below this threshold? ▾
Key Point

From an NLP perspective, the intrinsic dimensionality of GPT-4 embeddings can be analyzed as:

· Word semantic space: 300–800 dimensions
· Sentence-level semantics: 1,000–2,000 dimensions
· Sentence intent + discourse structure: 2,000–3,000 dimensions

Below 3,000 dimensions, it becomes impossible to distinguish sentences that are semantically similar from those that are merely syntactically similar. This is why 3,000 is the lower bound for "complete representation."

The value α ∈ [1.1, 1.4] also implies that LLM semantic energy has precisely this degree of structure — which incidentally explains why current LLMs can generate plausible text despite their structural limitations.

Mathematical Rigor 2 Q&A
Q11 Isn't equating embeddings with a "σ-additive family" a category mistake? Is this just a metaphor? ▾
AI's Critique

Calling a token sequence a Borel set requires a topology to be defined. "Embeddings as conditional expectation" is at best metaphorical, not a rigorous isomorphism. Embeddings are not measurable functions over a measure space.

Authors' Response

This is a mathematical definition, not a metaphor.

The identification of the embedding space with a σ-additive family generated by the Borel sets of a token sequence is mathematically correct. Without this identification, the entire framework of sequential approximation in autoregressive models — and the definition of learning representations over all contexts — would collapse.

The sequential update \(\mathcal{F}_t = \sigma(x_1,\ldots,x_t)\) is the canonical construction in probability theory for representing the growth of information over time. The reviewer's critique reflects a surface-level syntactic reading rather than a semantic understanding of the formalism — which is itself an illustration of the very problem WavePhaseNet aims to address.

Full technical details, including topology definitions, are provided in the final version of the paper.

Q12 There appear to be notation inconsistencies in the paper (X ∈ ℝ^{d×N} vs. X ∈ ℝ^{N×d}). Which is correct? ▾
Authors' Position

This notation inconsistency is acknowledged. The final version of the paper resolves this with unified conventions.

The theoretical substance is:
· Embedding dimension d treated as the "channel" axis
· Sequence length N treated as the axis along which DFT is applied

The transposition in notation reflects different conventions used in different sections and does not affect the content of the theory. The DFT index should run k = 0,…,N−1 when applied along the sequence direction; this is corrected in the final version.

Q13 How does cohomology regularization actually reduce contradictions in LLM reasoning? ▾

The cohomology section (0-cochains, coboundary energy, harmonic projection) is theoretically coherent, but the causal explanation for why this reduces LLM contradictions is weak.

Authors' Clarification

More precisely: the paper proposes cohomology as a means to compensate for one of the structural defects that causes hallucination, not to "reduce LLM contradictions" in general.

Role of each component:

· Local window Čech cohomology connection
→ Enables reasoning that is consistent with global semantic structure

· Coboundary energy (difference operator)
→ Computes differences in "energy" across the connections of meaning-preserving representations, quantifying the validity of inference

· Harmonic projection
→ Projects onto the "minimum energy state without spurious oscillations" (the harmonic form)
→ The projected vector preserves intent-level meaning from the low-frequency band, thereby reducing hallucination

Q14 How is the cohomology regularization fundamentally different from graph Laplacian regularization in Graph Attention Networks? ▾
Authors' Position
GAT graph Laplacian regularization is an approximate regularizer that encourages feature agreement among neighboring nodes — essentially a smoothness prior over a graph.

WavePhaseNet's cohomology regularization differs structurally:
① Based on Čech cohomology over a window covering structure
② Algebraic chain: 0-cochains → coboundary operator → 1-cochains
③ Orthogonal Hodge decomposition into harmonic, exact, and co-exact components
④ Joint optimization with DFT-derived global intent via the η-term in the unified loss

This is a topological "gluing of knowledge fragments" — fundamentally distinct from a neighborhood consistency penalty.

The review was conducted against an earlier draft. The final version includes figures, graphs, and theoretical proofs.
